## Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is beginning to reshape the Australian labour market, but the main near-term effect is not mass unemployment. Instead, AI is changing the tasks workers perform, especially in information-intensive and white-collar occupations, while increasing demand for workers who can combine digital capability with judgement, communication and domain expertise. Jobs and Skills Australia argues that generative AI is more likely to augment work than fully replace it at current levels of adoption, while the International Labour Organization finds that exposure is significant but uneven across occupations and income groups (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; International Labour Organization 2025).

This proposal recommends a three-part package: an AI Skills and Transition Fund, an AI Adjustment and Job Quality Compact, and a competition and diffusion agenda focused on SME access and concentration risks. Together, these measures would support productivity growth, reduce transition costs, and improve the distribution of AI’s benefits across workers, firms and regions.

## Problem Definition & Context

AI is changing work through several connected channels. First, it automates some routine cognitive tasks such as summarising, classification, drafting and workflow support. Second, it augments many jobs by improving speed, quality and consistency rather than removing the worker altogether. Third, it shifts skill demand toward digital fluency, critical reasoning, communication and oversight. Fourth, it can alter wage outcomes depending on who has access to complementary skills, training and capital. Fifth, it can reshape industry structure if firms with privileged access to data, compute and digital infrastructure gain persistent advantages (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; OECD 2024a).

For policy, this matters because Australia faces two problems at once. Labour productivity growth remains weak, so government has a strong interest in encouraging useful AI adoption. At the same time, the benefits of AI may be unevenly distributed if workers, small firms and exposed groups are left to adjust alone (Productivity Commission 2025). The policy challenge is therefore not whether AI should be adopted, but how to shape adoption so that productivity growth and labour-market fairness advance together.

## Analysis of Impacts

### Employment

The overall employment effect is likely to be mixed. Some tasks and roles will shrink, others will expand, and many jobs will be redesigned rather than eliminated. The ILO’s 2025 update does not predict uniform job destruction. Instead, it highlights different levels of occupational exposure and the likelihood of transformation, especially in clerical and digitised professional work (International Labour Organization 2025). Jobs and Skills Australia similarly notes that Australia has not yet seen broad economy-wide job loss from generative AI, but some structured and repetitive entry-level tasks may become more vulnerable as adoption deepens (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

### Tasks and skills

The most immediate effect is at the task level. Workers are increasingly required to verify outputs, exercise judgement, manage exceptions and integrate AI into workflows. This raises the value of complementary human skills rather than making labour irrelevant. Jobs and Skills Australia argues that capabilities such as communication, problem solving and accountability are becoming more important as AI takes over narrower routine functions (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

### Wages and distribution

The distributional effects are less certain, but they are important. IMF analysis suggests AI could increase labour income inequality where higher-income workers are better positioned to complement the technology and where capital owners capture a large share of productivity gains (International Monetary Fund 2024). OECD evidence is more cautious, finding no clear sign that AI has yet widened wage inequality between occupations in the data studied, although access to AI tools and training remains uneven (Georgieff 2024). The key policy issue is therefore who can adapt successfully, not just how fast AI spreads.

### Industrial structure

AI may also reshape industry structure. The OECD highlights risks across the generative AI value chain, especially around access to high-quality data, computing power and cloud infrastructure (OECD 2024a). The ACCC has likewise warned that digital markets can produce concentration and access problems that affect who captures value from technological change (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission 2025). If only large firms can adopt effectively, productivity gains will be narrower and labour-market opportunities more concentrated.

## Policy Options

### Option 1: AI Skills and Transition Fund

This option would establish a targeted national fund for reskilling, redeployment and modular capability-building. Priority should go to workers in high-exposure occupations, sectors undergoing rapid adoption, and regions with weaker access to training. Delivery should occur through TAFEs, universities and employer partnerships. This option performs strongly on efficiency and equity because it improves worker adaptability and helps labour move into complementary roles. However, training alone will not guarantee employment if local labour demand is weak (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

### Option 2: AI Adjustment and Job Quality Compact

This option would link AI adoption to worker consultation, internal training plans, redeployment pathways and reporting on workforce impacts in larger exposed firms. The purpose is to ensure AI is introduced as a productivity and job-design tool rather than only as a cost-cutting mechanism. This option performs strongly on fairness and job quality because it reduces the risk of unmanaged displacement and weak workplace governance. Its main drawback is implementation complexity, especially for medium-sized firms, so it should be phased and targeted rather than universal (OECD 2025; Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

### Option 3: Competition, Diffusion and Data Access Reform

This option would widen access to AI benefits by supporting SME adoption while monitoring concentration risks. Measures could include SME advisory support, diffusion grants, and stronger regulatory scrutiny of cloud, platform and data bottlenecks. This option matters because labour-market outcomes partly depend on market structure. If diffusion is narrow, the gains to productivity and wages will also be narrow. Its limitation is that competition reform is slower acting than direct labour-market intervention, so it is best used as a complement rather than a substitute (OECD 2024a; Australian Competition and Consumer Commission 2025).

| AI Skills and Transition Fund | High | High | Medium | Medium | Strong short-to-medium term option |

## Recommended Package & Implementation Plan

The recommended package combines all three options, with a clear sequence. The government should prioritise the AI Skills and Transition Fund and the AI Adjustment and Job Quality Compact in the first 12 months, while beginning competition and diffusion measures in parallel through existing digital policy processes.

This is the strongest package for three reasons. First, Australia needs productivity growth, and AI can support that if adoption is broad and skills-ready (Productivity Commission 2025). Second, the evidence suggests the main risk is uneven adjustment rather than immediate economy-wide unemployment (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; International Labour Organization 2025). Third, labour-market outcomes will be better if adoption is paired with training, consultation and broader diffusion.

### Timeline, responsibilities, costs and KPIs

**0–12 months**  
**Lead agency:** Department of Employment and Workplace Relations  
**Supporting agencies:** Jobs and Skills Australia, Treasury, state training systems  
**Actions:** launch a targeted AI Skills and Transition Fund; identify high-exposure occupations and regions; begin pilot training and redeployment programs  
**Indicative cost:** A$150 million over three years  
**KPIs:** enrolments, completion rates, redeployment rates, participation by regional and disadvantaged cohorts

**0–18 months**  
**Lead agency:** Department of Employment and Workplace Relations  
**Supporting partners:** employers, unions, Jobs and Skills Australia  
**Actions:** establish an AI Adjustment and Job Quality Compact with guidance on consultation, internal training and transition planning  
**Indicative cost:** A$40 million over three years  
**KPIs:** number of participating firms, worker consultation coverage, internal training uptake, proportion of displaced workers redeployed rather than exited

**12–36 months**  
**Lead agencies:** Treasury and ACCC  
**Supporting agencies:** digital economy and industry portfolios  
**Actions:** roll out SME AI advisory and diffusion support, integrate AI-related competition monitoring into existing digital market oversight  
**Indicative cost:** A$60 million over three years  
**KPIs:** SME AI uptake, regional participation, concentration indicators in key digital inputs, wage recovery after transition

Total indicative package cost is **A$250 million over three years**. This is justified as a targeted labour-market adjustment package rather than a general technology subsidy.

## Risks & Mitigations

The first risk is that training programs may not translate into job outcomes if they are poorly aligned with employer demand. This can be mitigated by targeting funding through Jobs and Skills Australia analysis and employer co-design.

The second risk is that AI adoption may widen inequality if high-income workers and large firms capture most of the benefits. This can be mitigated through targeted support for exposed workers and SMEs, and by monitoring access to key digital inputs.

The third risk is fragmented implementation across departments and jurisdictions. This can be mitigated by assigning clear lead agencies, publishing milestones, and reporting annually against the KPIs listed above.

## Conclusion

AI is likely to transform Australian work primarily through task change, skill reallocation and uneven competitive diffusion, not simple mass job replacement. That makes the appropriate policy response a managed transition strategy: invest in people, govern workplace adoption, and keep markets open enough for the benefits to spread. Australia should not try to stop AI adoption. It should shape AI adoption so that productivity growth and labour-market fairness advance together.

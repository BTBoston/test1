# Policy Proposal: Responding to AI's Impacts on the Labour Market

**To:** Deputy Secretary, Employment & Skills  
**From:** Boston Boynton  
**Date:** 21/04/2026  
**Subject:** A targeted policy package for AI-driven labour market adjustment in Australia

---

## Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is beginning to reshape the Australian labour market, but the main near-term effect is not wholesale job destruction. It is the reallocation of tasks within jobs, especially in information-intensive and white-collar work, alongside rising demand for workers who can combine digital capability with judgement, communication and domain expertise. Jobs and Skills Australia (JSA) finds that generative AI is more likely to augment work than replace it, but warns that impacts will vary across occupations, industries, regions and worker cohorts. The International Labour Organization (ILO) similarly finds that one in four jobs globally is exposed to some degree of generative AI transformation, with higher exposure in high-income economies. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

For Australia, the policy problem is twofold. First, labour productivity growth is weak, so government has a strong interest in supporting beneficial AI adoption. Second, the gains from AI may be unevenly distributed if workers, small firms and exposed cohorts are left to adjust alone. The Productivity Commission reports that Australia’s multifactor productivity growth has been almost non-existent in recent years, while the OECD warns that AI can raise productivity but also create risks around worker agency, privacy, discrimination and job loss if adoption is poorly governed. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

This brief recommends a three-part package:  
1. a targeted AI Skills and Transition Fund focused on exposed workers and sectors  
2. an AI Adjustment and Job Quality Compact that links adoption to worker consultation, transition support and workforce data  
3. a competition and diffusion agenda that helps SMEs access AI while reducing concentration risks around data, cloud and digital platforms.  

This package is recommended because it best balances productivity, equity and feasibility. It supports adaptation rather than trying to halt technological change, while making the labour-market transition more inclusive and less disruptive. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

## 1. Problem Definition: How AI is Changing Work

AI is changing work through five main channels. First, it automates some routine cognitive tasks such as drafting, summarising, classification and workflow support. Second, it augments many jobs by improving speed, quality and consistency, especially in knowledge-intensive work. Third, it shifts skill demand toward digital fluency, critical reasoning, communication and oversight. Fourth, it can alter bargaining power and wage outcomes depending on who has access to tools, training and complementary capital. Fifth, it can reshape industrial structure if firms with privileged access to data, compute and cloud infrastructure pull further ahead. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Australian evidence supports a task-based view. JSA’s 2025 Generative AI Capacity Study concludes that generative AI appears to have greater capacity to augment work than automate work at current task-level potential, and that effective outcomes depend on exposure, adoption and adaptation rather than exposure alone. It also finds that knowledge work, higher-level skills and information-based industries are more exposed to augmentation, while worker-led “shadow use” shows employees are already adopting AI tools independently, often without formal governance. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

This matters for policy because the transition is already underway but remains uneven. JSA notes that AI adoption is still early, varies widely across sectors, and is often constrained by capability gaps, organisational readiness and regulatory uncertainty. The OECD likewise finds that employer training and worker consultation are associated with better workplace outcomes where AI is introduced. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

## 2. Likely Impacts on Employment, Tasks, Wages and Industrial Structure

The overall employment effect is likely to be mixed. Some tasks and roles will shrink, others will expand, and many jobs will be redesigned. The ILO’s updated 2025 framework does not predict uniform job elimination; instead, it measures gradients of occupational exposure and emphasises transformation. JSA similarly reports that Australia has not yet seen clear evidence of broad entry-level job loss, although it warns that structured, repetitive entry-level tasks may become more vulnerable as adoption deepens. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

The most immediate effect will be on tasks. Workers will increasingly be expected to verify outputs, exercise judgement, manage exceptions and integrate AI into workflows. This increases the value of complementary human skills rather than eliminating the need for labour altogether. JSA explicitly notes that complementary human skills are increasingly in demand and that workers may need more judgement and accountability as entry-level and routine work changes. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

The distributional effects are less certain, but they are policy-relevant. IMF analysis argues that AI could increase labour income inequality where AI strongly complements higher-income workers and where capital owners capture a disproportionate share of gains. OECD evidence is more cautious: for the pre-GenAI period it finds no indication that AI affected wage inequality between occupations so far, though there is some evidence of lower wage inequality within occupations. The right policy conclusion is not complacency, but continued monitoring and intervention where access to tools and training is unequal. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

There are also cohort risks. JSA finds that women in Australia are marginally more exposed than men to both augmentation and automation because of occupational composition. It also stresses risks linked to digital divides, uneven training access and the possibility that AI compounds pre-existing disadvantage. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Finally, AI may change industry structure. OECD analysis warns that competition risks can emerge across the generative AI value chain because quality data and computing power are vital inputs. The ACCC has also highlighted emerging competition and consumer issues in cloud computing and generative AI and has recommended both ongoing monitoring of emerging digital technologies and a whole-of-government regulatory approach. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

## 3. Policy Options

### Option 1: AI Skills and Transition Fund

The first option is a targeted national fund for reskilling and redeployment. It should prioritise workers in high-exposure occupations, industries undergoing rapid adoption, and regions with weaker training access. Delivery should be through TAFEs, universities, employer partnerships and modular short-form programs. JSA’s report strongly supports accessible short-form training, including micro-credentials and occupation-mobility training into high-growth roles, and argues governments cannot afford to ignore flexible learning models during accelerated technological change. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

This option is strong on productivity and equity because it helps workers move into complementary roles rather than leaving adjustment to firms or individuals. Its weakness is that training alone does not guarantee job transition, especially where workers face income constraints or where local labour demand is weak. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

### Option 2: AI Adjustment and Job Quality Compact

The second option is a compact linking AI adoption to worker consultation, job redesign and transition support. For firms above a threshold size in exposed sectors, this could include consultation requirements, internal training plans, redeployment pathways, and reporting on workforce impacts. This is justified because OECD evidence shows training and worker consultation are associated with better outcomes, while JSA warns that unmanaged “shadow use” can shift governance and risk onto workers and produce fragmented adoption. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

This option is strong on job quality and fairness. It reduces the risk that AI becomes a top-down cost-cutting exercise and instead encourages adoption that actually improves productivity and work design. Its weakness is implementation complexity, particularly for medium-sized firms, so it should be phased and targeted rather than universal. :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}

### Option 3: Competition, Diffusion and Data Access Reform

The third option is to widen access to AI’s benefits by reducing concentration risks and helping smaller firms adopt the technology. This should include SME advisory support, diffusion grants, interoperability and portability-friendly settings where appropriate, and stronger regulatory monitoring of cloud, platforms and AI-related bottlenecks. The OECD and ACCC both warn that data access, cloud dependence, talent concentration and barriers to key inputs can shape who captures value from AI. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

This option is essential because labour-market outcomes depend partly on market structure. If only large firms can adopt effectively, productivity gains will be narrower and regional job opportunities weaker. Its limitation is that competition reform works more slowly than training or adjustment policy, so it should complement rather than replace labour-market intervention. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

## 4. Recommendation

The recommended package combines all three options, but with a clear sequence. The government should prioritise Option 1 and Option 2 in the first 12 months, while beginning Option 3 in parallel through existing competition and digital policy processes.

This is the best package for three reasons. First, Australia needs productivity growth, and AI can help if adoption is broad and skills-ready. Second, the evidence suggests the main risk is uneven adjustment, not immediate economy-wide unemployment. Third, labour-market outcomes will be better if adoption is paired with training, consultation and competitive diffusion. A policy mix focused only on reskilling would be too narrow; a mix focused only on regulation would be too slow; and a hands-off approach would risk widening inequality and wasting productivity potential. :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}

## 5. Implementation

Within 12 months, the Department of Employment and Workplace Relations should establish an AI Skills and Transition Fund with Jobs and Skills Australia identifying priority occupations, sectors and regions. Early funding should target modular training, redeployment pathways and capability uplift in sectors with high exposure but low readiness. :contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}

In parallel, the Australian Government should convene an AI Adjustment and Job Quality Compact involving employers, unions, JSA and relevant state training systems. The aim should be practical guidance on consultation, internal training, transition planning and workforce measurement. JSA’s proposed national stewardship approach provides a strong institutional rationale for this kind of coordinated mechanism. :contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}

Over 12 to 36 months, Treasury, the ACCC and relevant digital regulators should integrate AI-related competition monitoring into existing digital market frameworks, with a particular focus on cloud, data, interoperability and SME access. Success should be measured not only by adoption rates, but by re-employment outcomes, wage recovery after transition, SME uptake, and whether productivity gains are spreading beyond frontier firms. :contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21}

## Conclusion

AI is likely to transform Australian work primarily through task change, skill reallocation and uneven competitive diffusion, not simple mass job replacement. That makes the right policy response a managed transition strategy: invest in people, govern workplace adoption, and keep markets open enough for the benefits to spread. Australia should not try to stop AI adoption. It should shape it so that productivity growth and labour-market fairness advance together. :contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}

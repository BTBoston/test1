# Policy Proposal: Responding to AI's Impacts on the Labour Market

**To:** Deputy Secretary, Employment & Skills  
**From:** Boston Boynton  
**Date:** 21/04/2026  
**Subject:** A policy package to respond to AI-driven labour market change

## Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is reshaping the labour market by changing how tasks are performed, altering demand for different skills, affecting wage distribution, and shifting industry structure. While AI can improve productivity and create new forms of work, it also risks displacing routine tasks, widening inequality, and concentrating market power in large firms with access to data and compute. This proposal recommends a three-part policy package: a targeted national reskilling strategy, stronger labour-market adjustment supports, and a complementary competition and data framework. Together, these policies would help workers transition into new roles, reduce the costs of displacement, and ensure that productivity gains are more broadly shared across the economy.

## 1. Problem Definition: How AI is Changing Work

AI is not simply replacing whole occupations. More often, it changes the **tasks** within jobs. Routine, predictable, and information-based tasks are especially vulnerable to automation, while jobs requiring interpersonal judgement, physical adaptability, and complex decision-making are more likely to be transformed than eliminated. This means AI is likely to create uneven effects across sectors and occupations.

The key channels through which AI is changing work are:

1. **Task automation**  
   AI can perform repetitive cognitive tasks such as summarising documents, processing claims, coding simple scripts, and generating standardised content.

2. **Task augmentation**  
   In many roles, AI increases worker productivity rather than replacing workers entirely. For example, it can support analysts, teachers, customer service staff, and health professionals.

3. **Wage polarisation**  
   AI may increase demand for high-skill workers who can use and supervise these systems, while reducing demand for some middle-skill routine roles.

4. **Industrial restructuring**  
   Firms that adopt AI effectively may gain scale advantages, potentially increasing concentration in some industries and changing competitive dynamics.

5. **Skill mismatch and transition pressure**  
   Workers displaced from declining tasks may not immediately possess the capabilities needed for growing roles.

These changes create a policy challenge: government must support productivity growth while reducing transition costs and protecting social cohesion.

## 2. Likely Impacts on the Workforce

### Employment

The overall effect of AI on employment is likely to be mixed. Some jobs will decline, some will expand, and many will change in content. Administrative support, clerical roles, and some customer-facing routine service positions are particularly exposed. At the same time, demand may grow in digital services, advanced manufacturing, health, education, green transition industries, and AI-complementary occupations.

### Tasks

The most significant immediate impact is likely to occur at the task level. Workers will increasingly need to supervise, interpret, validate, and integrate AI-generated outputs. This shifts the value of human labour toward judgement, communication, problem solving, and accountability.

### Wages

AI may raise productivity and wages for workers who can use it effectively, but it may also suppress wages in roles where AI substitutes for core tasks. Without intervention, the gains from AI adoption may flow disproportionately to capital owners and highly skilled workers, increasing income inequality.

### Industrial Structure

AI may favour larger firms with better access to proprietary data, infrastructure, and technical expertise. This could reduce competition and make it harder for smaller firms and regional employers to benefit from AI adoption. If this occurs, labour-market opportunities may become more concentrated geographically and sectorally.

## 3. Policy Options

## Option 1: Targeted National Reskilling and Lifelong Learning Program

Government could establish a targeted AI transition training program focused on workers in occupations with high task exposure. This should prioritise short-form, stackable credentials in digital literacy, AI tool use, data handling, critical thinking, and sector-specific technical skills.

**Advantages**
- Improves worker adaptability
- Supports movement into growing occupations
- Helps spread AI productivity gains more broadly

**Limitations**
- Training alone does not guarantee employment
- Programs may be ineffective if not aligned with employer demand
- Workers with financial or time constraints may struggle to participate

## Option 2: Strengthened Labour-Market Adjustment Support

Government could expand adjustment assistance for workers affected by AI-related restructuring. This may include wage insurance, stronger job-matching services, career counselling, relocation assistance, and temporary income support tied to retraining.

**Advantages**
- Reduces the personal cost of displacement
- Helps workers re-enter employment faster
- Supports regional and sectoral adjustment

**Limitations**
- Can be fiscally expensive
- Requires accurate targeting
- Risks becoming reactive rather than forward-looking

## Option 3: Competition and Data Policy Reform

Government could strengthen competition policy and data access arrangements to reduce the risk that AI adoption leads to excessive industry concentration. This could include data portability, interoperability standards, support for SME digital adoption, and closer scrutiny of anti-competitive conduct in AI-intensive markets.

**Advantages**
- Encourages wider diffusion of AI benefits
- Supports smaller firms and innovation
- Reduces the risk of winner-takes-most market outcomes

**Limitations**
- Effects may take time to materialise
- Complex to regulate effectively
- Requires coordination across multiple departments

## 4. Recommended Policy Package

This proposal recommends a combined package of all three options, with the strongest emphasis on **targeted reskilling** and **labour-market adjustment**, supported by **competition and data reforms**.

The most effective response to AI is not to slow adoption, but to shape it. A worker-centred response should help individuals adapt while also ensuring market conditions allow productivity gains to diffuse across firms and industries.

The recommended package is:

- A national AI skills and transition fund targeting exposed occupations and regions
- Expanded employment services and adjustment assistance for displaced workers
- SME-focused AI adoption support linked to competition and data-access reforms

This package is preferable because it addresses both the **supply side** of labour adaptation and the **structural market conditions** that determine who benefits from AI.

## 5. Implementation

The proposal should be implemented over three years.

**Year 1**
- Identify high-exposure occupations and regions
- Launch pilot retraining partnerships with TAFEs, universities, and employers
- Expand labour-market data collection on AI exposure

**Year 2**
- Scale training and transition supports nationally
- Introduce SME AI adoption grants
- Begin targeted competition and data policy reforms

**Year 3**
- Evaluate program outcomes
- Refine support based on employment and wage effects
- Embed successful measures into long-term workforce policy

## 6. Conclusion

AI is likely to transform work through task change, wage pressure, and industrial restructuring rather than simple mass unemployment. Government policy should therefore focus on helping workers adapt, cushioning adjustment costs, and ensuring the gains from AI are not captured by only a small number of firms or high-skill workers. A combined package of reskilling, labour-market transition support, and competition/data reform offers the most balanced and effective response.

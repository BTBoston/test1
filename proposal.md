# AI and the Australian Labour Market: Policy Proposal

## Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is reshaping the Australian labour market, but the near-term risk is not mass unemployment. The stronger concern is uneven task change across information-intensive and white-collar work. AI can automate routine cognitive tasks while increasing demand for digital capability, judgement and communication. Jobs and Skills Australia argues that generative AI is more likely to augment work than fully replace it, while the International Labour Organization finds that exposure is significant but uneven across occupations and income groups (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; International Labour Organization 2025).

This proposal recommends a three-part package: an AI Skills and Redeployment Fund, a Workplace AI Governance and Job Quality Compact, and an AI Diffusion, SME Adoption and Competition Reform Package.

## Problem Definition and Context

AI is changing work through automation, augmentation, skill shifts and market concentration. It can automate summarising, classification, drafting and workflow support, but also improve the speed and quality of many jobs without removing the worker. Wage and employment outcomes will depend on who has access to training, complementary capital and digital infrastructure (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; OECD 2024a).

Australia faces two linked problems. Weak productivity growth means useful AI adoption should be encouraged. However, AI benefits may be unevenly distributed if workers, small firms and exposed groups adjust alone (Productivity Commission 2025). The challenge is to shape adoption so that productivity and labour-market fairness advance together.

## Analysis of Impacts

The employment effect is likely to be mixed. Some tasks and roles will shrink, others will expand, and many jobs will be redesigned rather than removed. The ILO highlights occupational exposure, especially in clerical and digitised professional work (International Labour Organization 2025). Jobs and Skills Australia similarly finds that Australia has not yet seen broad economy-wide job loss from generative AI, although repetitive entry-level tasks may become more vulnerable as adoption deepens (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025).

The most immediate effect is at the task level. Workers must increasingly verify outputs, manage exceptions and integrate AI into workflows, raising the value of communication, problem solving and accountability. Distributional effects are also important. IMF analysis suggests AI could increase labour income inequality where higher-income workers are better positioned to complement the technology and where capital owners capture productivity gains (International Monetary Fund 2024). OECD evidence is more cautious, finding no clear sign that AI has yet widened wage inequality between occupations, although access to AI tools and training remains uneven (Georgieff 2024).

AI may also reshape industry structure. The OECD highlights risks around access to data, computing power and cloud infrastructure (OECD 2024a). The ACCC has warned that digital markets can create concentration and access problems (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission 2025). If only large firms adopt AI effectively, productivity gains and labour-market opportunities will be narrower.

## Policy Options

### Option 1: AI Skills and Redeployment Fund

This option would establish a targeted national fund for workers whose roles are most exposed to AI-driven task change. It would provide subsidised micro-credentials, paid training leave, wage subsidies for redeployment and regional training hubs. Priority should go to clerical, administrative, customer service, finance support, legal support and other task-based roles where generative AI can automate or reshape routine work.

Delivery should occur through TAFEs, universities, industry bodies and employer partnerships. Funding should be linked to completion rates, redeployment rates, wage retention and movement into AI-complementary roles such as AI supervision, compliance, data interpretation and workflow redesign. This option performs strongly on efficiency and equity, but training alone does not guarantee employment if local labour demand is weak. It should therefore be connected to employer demand and regional workforce plans.

### Option 2: Workplace AI Governance and Job Quality Compact

This option would require larger firms in exposed sectors to complete an AI workforce impact assessment before deploying AI systems that materially affect workers. This should apply where AI is used for hiring, rostering, performance monitoring, task allocation, promotion, redundancy decisions or customer service automation.

Firms would need to identify affected roles, estimate likely task change or displacement, consult workers or representatives, provide internal training plans, create redeployment pathways and report annually on workforce impacts. The aim is not to block AI adoption, but to ensure it is introduced as a productivity and job-design tool rather than only as a labour cost-cutting mechanism.

The European Union provides a useful comparison. The EU AI Act uses a risk-based model and treats employment-related AI as high risk, including systems used for recruitment, worker selection, promotion, termination, task allocation and performance evaluation. Article 26 also requires employers deploying high-risk AI systems to inform workers’ representatives and affected workers before use. Australia does not need to copy the EU model exactly, but the principle is relevant: workplace AI should not be treated as a normal software upgrade when it affects hiring, monitoring, promotion, dismissal or job quality. This option performs strongly on fairness, although compliance complexity means it should begin with large firms.

### Option 3: AI Diffusion, SME Adoption and Competition Reform

This option would prevent AI productivity gains from being captured mainly by large firms and dominant digital platforms. Measures could include SME AI adoption vouchers, TAFE and university-based AI productivity clinics, industry advisory services, data portability standards and stronger ACCC monitoring of cloud, platform and data bottlenecks.

This option matters because labour-market outcomes depend on who can access and benefit from AI. If adoption is concentrated among large firms, productivity and wage gains may also become concentrated, while SMEs fall behind due to weaker technical capacity and limited capital. This option supports long-term productivity and competition, but it is slower acting than direct labour-market intervention.

## Recommended Package and Implementation

The recommended response is a combined package led by Option 2, supported by Options 1 and 3. Option 2 should lead because the most immediate risk is unmanaged workplace adjustment, not mass unemployment. If firms adopt AI without consultation, training or transparency, productivity gains may come at the cost of job quality and worker trust.

Implementation should occur in three stages. In the first 12 months, the Department of Employment and Workplace Relations, Jobs and Skills Australia and state training systems should launch the AI Skills and Redeployment Fund. Indicative cost: A$150 million over three years. KPIs should include enrolments, completion rates, redeployment rates and wage retention.

Over 0-18 months, the Department of Employment and Workplace Relations should establish the Workplace AI Governance and Job Quality Compact with employers, unions and Jobs and Skills Australia. Indicative cost: A$40 million over three years. KPIs should include participating firms, worker consultation coverage, internal training uptake and the proportion of affected workers redeployed rather than exited.

Over 12-36 months, Treasury and the ACCC should roll out SME AI advisory support and integrate AI-related competition monitoring into digital market oversight. Indicative cost: A$60 million over three years. KPIs should include SME AI uptake, regional participation, concentration indicators in key digital inputs and wage recovery after transition. Total indicative package cost is A$250 million over three years.

## Risks and Mitigations

Key risks are weak training-to-job outcomes, inequality and fragmented implementation. These can be mitigated by linking funding to employer demand, targeting exposed workers and SMEs, assigning clear lead agencies, publishing milestones and reporting annually against KPIs.

## Conclusion

AI is likely to transform Australian work through task change, skill reallocation and uneven competitive diffusion, not simple mass job replacement. The best response is a managed transition strategy: invest in workers, govern workplace adoption and keep markets open enough for the benefits to spread. Australia should shape AI adoption so that productivity growth and labour-market fairness advance together.
